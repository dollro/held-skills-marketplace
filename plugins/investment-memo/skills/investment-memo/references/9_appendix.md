## Appendix B: BCSA Technology Adoption Framework

> *Source: NAP BCSA Technology Adoption Model.*

The BCSA framework assesses whether a product achieves adoption readiness across four dimensions. It is applied in Chapter 8.6 as a cross-cutting IC assessment.

**CRITICAL INSIGHT:** Many deep-tech startups prove a 10x technical advantage (high score on criterion 5.2) but fail to achieve adoption because the product is not Simpler or Available. This is the "great technology, no sales" trap. The BCSA framework systematically identifies these blind spots.

### Framework

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│        B+C = Business Case          │
│  ┌──────────┐  ┌──────────┐        │
│  │  Better   │  │  Cheaper  │        │
│  └──────────┘  └──────────┘        │
│        S+A = Ease of Adoption       │
│  ┌──────────┐  ┌──────────┐        │
│  │  Simpler  │  │ Available │        │
│  └──────────┘  └──────────┘        │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Four Dimensions

| Dimension | Axis | Core Question | Assessment Focus |
|-----------|:---:|---|---|
| **B — Better** | Business Case (B+C) | Does the product deliver measurable, quantifiable value for the **decision-maker** (not the engineer)? | Value and impact for customers, ESG factors, quality/uptime/performance, ease of use, design/brand |
| **C — Cheaper** | Business Case (B+C) | Is the product cheaper from the perspective of the **relevant decision-maker** — CAPEX, OPEX, TCO, or NOI? | Initial CAPEX, fees (initial + recurring), total installation cost, total lifetime costs. Critical: Which cost metric matters to the decision-maker at this stage of the value chain? |
| **S — Simpler** | Ease of Adoption (S+A) | Is the product at least as easy to understand, integrate, and operate as the existing solution? | Comprehensibility, simulation/forecasting capability pre-purchase, installation and integration effort, required changes at the customer, operating/service complexity, compatibility with existing and future systems |
| **A — Available** | Ease of Adoption (S+A) | Can the company deliver in the required volumes, timeframes, and with the necessary service — comparable to existing suppliers? | Delivery capacity in quantity and timing, installation/configuration personnel (own or third-party), service and support across the full lifecycle, warranties at competitive level |

**Key insight:** B and C alone are insufficient for adoption. If the product is Better and Cheaper but neither Simpler nor Available, adoption rates remain low. Conversely, if S+A are at parity with competition while B+C are strong, the purchase decision becomes a "no-brainer."

**Key principle:** S and A do not need to exceed competitive parity — they must match it. Over-investment in S+A beyond parity does not generate additional conversion.

### Calibration Case Studies

**Wiferion (S-dimension calibration):**

Wiferion developed wireless charging solutions for industrial vehicles (forklifts, AGVs). Initial product: technically superior but different form factor and higher price than wired solutions → low adoption. Redesigned product: matched size and price of existing wired solutions → BCSA achieved → 143% CAGR → exit to Tesla.

**Lesson:** S+A parity unlocked adoption even though the core technology was unchanged. Product redesign to match incumbent form factor was the key intervention.

**Microshade (Value chain calibration):**

Microshade produces automated shading systems for buildings to reduce energy consumption. Initial GTM: Sold through architects/engineers to building owners → 2% conversion (architects evaluated "Better" based on aesthetics, not energy savings; building owners never saw the value proposition). Redesigned GTM: Bypassed traditional value chain to sell directly to building owners emphasizing energy cost savings → conversion improved from 2% to 25–30%.

**Lesson:** Finding the value chain stage with highest Natural Relevance is critical. The product was unchanged — only the GTM targeting changed.

### BCSA Benchmarks

| Metric | Strong BCSA Signal | Weak BCSA Signal |
|--------|:---:|:---:|
| Customer conversion rate | >65% | <30% |
| Gross Profit / Cost of Engagement ratio | Above-average | Below-average |
| Sales cycle length | Shorter than competitors | Longer than competitors |
| Average deal size vs. initial pilot | Growing (expansion) | Stagnant or shrinking |

## Appendix M: Deep-Tech Matrix Framework (HTGF DTM)

> *Source: HTGF Deep-Tech Matrix, Berger/Joeressen/Fiebig, 2025. Based on analysis of >200 deep-tech investments and 15 late-stage investor interviews.*

The Deep-Tech Matrix (originally developed as the HTGF DTM) is a two-dimensional framework for assessing deep-tech investment readiness and stage-appropriate expectations. It is applied in Chapter 8.7 for deal positioning and trajectory analysis.

**Critical insight:** "Series A" in deep-tech is not "Series A" in SaaS. Deep-tech startups require >48 months to reach revenue scale (vs. ~24 months for SaaS). The financing taxonomy is shifted by approximately 2 rounds: what qualifies as "Seed" in SaaS corresponds to "Pre-Seed" in deep-tech.

### Matrix Visualization

```
Technology Readiness Level (Y-axis)
  9 ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  8 │                              Series B    │ ☆
  7 │ Engineering          Series A            │
  6 │ Execution                                │
  5 │ TRL              Seed                    │
  4 │                                          │
  3 │ Proof of    Pre-Seed                     │
  2 │ Concept                                  │
  1 │ Scientific  (No Money Land)              │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
      1       2       3       4       5       6   Market Certainty
      unclear niche   estab.  high    main-   block-
                      niche   momentum stream  buster
      ←── unproven ──→       ←── proven ──→
```

### Y-Axis: Technology Readiness Level (TRL 1–9)

| TRL Cluster | TRL | Description |
|-------------|:---:|-------------|
| Scientific Breakthrough | 1–2 | Basic research, observed principles |
| Proof of Concept | 3–4 | Technology concept validated, laboratory validation |
| Demonstration | 5–6 | Validation in relevant / operational environment |
| Engineering Execution | 7–8 | System demonstration / qualified in operational environment |
| Production Ready | 9 | Technology market-ready, series production |

### X-Axis: Market Certainty for a Huge Market (1–6)

| Level | Label | Description |
|:---:|---|---|
| 1 | Unclear if there is a market at all | No identifiable market application |
| 2 | Niche market | Small, limited market identified |
| 3 | Established niche, first indications of scaling | Niche established, early scaling signals |
| 4 | High momentum, clear indications of scaling | Strong momentum, clear scaling indicators |
| 5 | Mainstream Market (>€10B) | Mainstream market, validated size >€10B |
| 6 | Blockbuster Market (>€100B) | Blockbuster market >€100B |

### Matrix Zones and Investability

| Zone | TRL Range | Market Range | Characteristics | Investability |
|------|:---:|:---:|---|:---:|
| **No Money Land** | TRL 1–3 at Market 1–2 | Low × Low | "Double Bet" — both technology AND market unproven. Also: TRL >6 without identifiable market | Not VC-fundable |
| **Pre-Seed** | TRL 3–5 | Market 2–4 | Use-case hypothesis with initial technical feasibility and IP | €1–5M rounds |
| **Seed** | TRL 4–6 | Market 3–5 | Customer validation through LOIs, pilots, PoC. Prototype functional | €3–10M rounds |
| **Series A** | TRL 5–7 | Market 4–6 | Science risk resolved, MVP available, engineering execution | >€10M rounds |
| **Series B** | TRL 7–9 | Market 5–6 | Product ready for scaling. Proof of repeatability, significant revenue | >€20M rounds |

**"No Money Land" Critical Check:** All surveyed late-stage investors categorically exclude the "double bet" — simultaneously unproven technology AND unvalidated market. A company with TRL >6 but Market Certainty 1–2 is also in "No Money Land" — even TRL-9 products without an attractive market cannot raise VC capital.

### Stage Expectations (Benchmark Table)

| Dimension | Pre-Seed | Seed | Series A | Series B |
|-----------|----------|------|----------|----------|
| **Tech** | TRL 4, unique science, IP initiated | TRL 5, small-scale demo, first IP granted | TRL 5/6, MVP ready, science proven, additional IP | TRL 8/9, product ready for scaling |
| **Market & Product** | Application hypothesis, potential disruption | Disruptive target application identified, huge market | Application & pain confirmed incl. quantified value | Data-based GTM defined, expansion strategy |
| **Commercial** | — | Business case discussed with customers, buying center understood | PMF understood and quantified, paid PoCs >€100K | PMF proven, USP & value proven, pipeline >€10M |
| **Team** | Science covered, product leader candidate | Key contributors hired across org-chart | Org prepared for engineering execution, C-level extension | Org prepared for scaling, key people ready |
| **Round Volume** | €1–5M | €3–10M | >€10M | >€20M |
| **Pre-Money** | €3–10M | €5–30M (1.2–2.0× post-money) | €12–80M (1.5–2.0× post-money) | >€100M |
| **Revenue + Orders** | €0 | €0–0.5M | €0.1–2M | >€5M |
| **Public Funding Share** | 50% | 50% | 25% | 10% |

> *Deep-tech timeline: SaaS scales from ~24 months, deep tech from >48 months — comparable after 7 years. Funding taxonomy is shifted by ~2 rounds.*

## Appendix V: Verdict Scale & Calibration

The 6-point verdict scale replaces the previous dual system (Conviction Level + Recommendation). It forces a clear position and makes memos comparable across deals.

### Verdict Scale

| Verdict | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **STRONG PASS** | Fundamental problems — broken unit economics, unfixable market issues, team red flags, or the opportunity simply isn't venture-scale. Don't waste IC time. |
| **PASS** | Decent team or idea but significant concerns unlikely to be resolved. Missing PMF signals at a stage where expected. Competitive dynamics unfavorable. |
| **LEAN PASS** | Interesting but not compelling enough. Market may be real but the team is B+, or the product is good but the moat is thin. Worth tracking but not investing today. |
| **LEAN INVEST** | Promising across most dimensions with some open questions. Key Questions (Chapter 1) need answering — if answers are good, this moves to INVEST. |
| **INVEST** | Strong team, real traction, large market, defensible position. Risk/reward clearly favorable. Advocate for this at IC. |
| **STRONG INVEST** | Exceptional across the board — the kind of deal where if you don't invest, you'll regret it. Rare. Maybe 1–2 a year. |

### Stage-Specific Calibration

**At Seed:** You're betting on the team and the insight. Forgive missing metrics, but demand clarity of thinking and evidence of founder-market fit. The question is: "Can these people figure it out?"

**At Series A:** You're betting on early proof. Expect real retention data, repeatable GTM, and a credible path to €10M+ ARR. The question is: "Is this working and can it scale?"

### Analyst Instincts

- Market size claims in pitch decks are almost always inflated. Apply your own bottom-up sizing and compare. If there's a 5x+ gap, flag it.
- "AI-powered" is not a moat. "We trained on proprietary dataset X that took 3 years to assemble" might be.
- Revenue growth rate matters more than absolute revenue at seed/A. A company at €50K MRR growing 25% MoM is more interesting than one at €500K MRR growing 5% MoM.
- The best seed investments often look weird or contrarian. Don't penalize unconventional approaches — interrogate them, but give them room.
- Negative signals that matter more than people think: founders who can't articulate why customers churn, pitch decks that avoid showing retention curves, "we have no competitors" claims.

## Appendix T: Term Sheet & Negotiation Phase

> **Not scored at IC stage.** This appendix provides the assessment framework for deal terms to be used **after IC approval**, during the term sheet negotiation and signing phase. The IC decides whether to make a proposal; these criteria guide the terms of that proposal.

**Assessment guidance (junior analyst checklist) — applies to all T.1–T.4 sections:**

1. **Gather the proposed terms:** Obtain the draft term sheet from the founders / lead investor. If no term sheet exists yet, document the verbally discussed terms. Flag any terms that are missing or ambiguous — these are negotiation leverage points.

2. **Benchmark against market standard:** For each term in T.1–T.4, compare the proposed term against the "Market Standard (Seed DACH, 2025)" column. Mark each as ☐ Standard (matches market) or flag deviations. Not every deviation is bad — but every deviation must be *understood and justified*.

3. **Assess T.1 (Instrument & Preferences):**
   - Identify the instrument type. Priced equity is cleanest; convertibles and SAFEs introduce valuation cap complexity. For convertibles: check discount rate, cap, interest, maturity, and qualified financing trigger.
   - Check liquidation preference: 1x non-participating is standard. Anything above 1x at Seed is a red flag. Participating preferred ("double dip") significantly disadvantages common shareholders — push back hard.
   - Check anti-dilution: Weighted average broad-based is standard. Full ratchet is founder-hostile and signals adversarial investor dynamics.
   - If prior rounds exist: map the full preference stack (waterfall). Do earlier investors' preferences stack on top? Model a downside exit scenario (e.g., 1.5x the round size) and check what common shareholders receive.

4. **Assess T.2 (Governance & Control):**
   - Board composition: 1 investor seat at Seed is standard. Assess whether the proposed board structure gives investors blocking control (majority) — at Seed this is unusual and should be flagged.
   - Protective provisions: Review the consent catalog. Standard items: new equity issuance, debt above threshold, M&A, change of business, related-party transactions above €100K. Non-standard additions: hiring/firing below C-level, travel budgets, individual expense approvals — these are over-controlling and damage founder trust.
   - Information rights: Quarterly financials + annual audit is standard. Monthly reporting is acceptable if the company has the finance infrastructure. If reporting requirements are onerous, assess whether the company can realistically comply.
   - Pro-rata: Full pro-rata for follow-on rounds is non-negotiable. If absent, negotiate it in.
   - ESOP/VSOP: 10–15% pool is standard. If no pool exists, insist it is created pre-close (dilution shared by all shareholders, not just post-money).

5. **Assess T.3 (Vesting & Lock-In):**
   - Check whether founder shares are subject to reverse vesting. If founders are fully vested pre-investment, this is a significant concern — they can walk away with their full equity allocation.
   - Standard: 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff. Assess acceleration triggers: single trigger (acceleration on acquisition) vs. double trigger (acceleration only if both acquisition AND termination occur). Double trigger is investor-friendly; single trigger is founder-friendly.
   - Review good leaver / bad leaver provisions. Bad leaver (termination for cause, voluntary departure) should result in forfeiture at nominal value. Good leaver (termination without cause, death, disability) should result in fair value for vested shares.
   - German-specific: Ensure vesting structure is compatible with German tax treatment (Zukunftsfinanzierungsgesetz 2024 provisions for employee equity).

6. **Assess T.4 (Conditions Precedent):**
   - Walk through each CP item. For each: Is it already satisfied, in progress, or not yet started?
   - IP assignment is critical: ALL IP must be exclusively assigned to the company (not personally held by founders). Check employment contracts for IP assignment clauses. If founders developed core IP at a university or prior employer, verify clean separation (FTO from 3.3).
   - D&O insurance: Verify it's in place before capital disbursement. Personal liability exposure without D&O is unacceptable.
   - Cap table cleanliness: The cap table must match the Handelsregister (commercial register). Any discrepancies must be resolved pre-close.
   - Timeline: Estimate the realistic timeline to satisfy all CPs. If any CP requires >60 days, flag the risk of deal delay and cash bridge needs.

7. **Negotiate strategically:** Identify your 3 non-negotiables (typically: board seat, pro-rata, reverse vesting). Identify 2–3 terms where you have flexibility to concede. Lead negotiations with the non-negotiables and use the flexible terms as trading chips. Document the rationale for any non-standard terms accepted.
