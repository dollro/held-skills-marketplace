## 3. Technology, IP & Industrialization

> **Assessment Pillars:** Technology + Legal & Taxes | **Weight:** Seed 20% / Series A 20%
>
> This chapter covers the full arc from innovation depth through IP protection to production scalability. The central question for deep-tech: Is this a **Product** or a **Feature**? A product creates its own market and defensibility. A feature is an incremental improvement vulnerable to commoditization or platform absorption.

### 3.1 — Innovation Depth [0–5] 🚩 RED FLAG

The technology is a genuine, defensible innovation with standalone market potential — it creates a new category or meaningfully redefines performance parameters (10x improvement). It is not a wrapper, integration layer, or marginal optimization that a well-resourced incumbent could replicate within 18 months with a dedicated team.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Ask the founders to quantify their improvement over the status quo along the dimension that matters most to the buyer (cost, speed, accuracy, safety, etc.). Look for 10x claims — then verify independently.
- Apply the "well-resourced incumbent test": Could Google / Siemens / Bosch / a well-funded startup replicate this with a dedicated team within 18 months? If yes, it's a feature.
- Check if the technology creates a new workflow or replaces an existing one. New workflows = product. Marginal improvements to existing workflows = feature.
- For AI/ML companies specifically: Is the core value in proprietary data, novel model architecture, or domain-specific fine-tuning? Pure prompt-engineering layers or API wrappers over foundation models score 0–1.
- Review technical publications: Is this based on genuinely novel research, or an application of well-known techniques?

> *Deep-tech litmus test: Could a well-resourced incumbent replicate this within 18 months with a dedicated team? If yes, it's a feature.*

---

### 3.2 — TRL & DTM Position [0–5] 🚩 RED FLAG

The current Technology Readiness Level (TRL) meets or exceeds the stage-appropriate threshold (TRL ≥4 for Seed, TRL ≥6 for Series A) supported by reproducible evidence, and the development roadmap to the next TRL milestones is time-bound, budgeted, and technically credible. The company occupies a favorable position on the Deep-Tech Matrix (Technology Readiness Level × Market Certainty for a Huge Market; cf. HTGF DTM, Berger/Joeressen/Fiebig 2025) and avoids the "double bet" — simultaneously unproven technology AND unvalidated market. The investment carries at most one-dimensional risk: either early technology in an established market OR proven technology in an emerging market. The progression path through the matrix is clearly defined, with concrete milestones that move the company from its current zone into the next financing zone.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**TRL Assessment:**

| TRL | Description | Typical Evidence |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|
| 1–3 | Basic research / proof of concept | Academic papers, lab results |
| 4 | Component validation in lab | Prototype with test data |
| 5 | System validation in relevant environment | Integrated prototype testing |
| 6 | Demonstration in operational environment | Pilot deployment with real users |
| 7–9 | System complete / operational / qualified | Commercial deployment data |

- Ask the team to self-assess their TRL with evidence for each level claimed. Then independently verify: Has TRL 4 been demonstrated with reproducible test data, or is it a lab one-off?
- Request the development roadmap: What specific milestones (with dates and budgets) take them from current TRL to next level? Is each milestone tied to a use-of-proceeds budget line?
- Cross-check with external technical validation (3.5): Does an independent expert confirm the claimed TRL?
- For hardware: Also assess Manufacturing Readiness Level (MRL) and Commercial Readiness Level (CRL) in parallel — a TRL 7 product with MRL 3 means the product works but can't be manufactured. These readiness levels must advance roughly in sync.

**Deep-Tech Matrix Positioning:**

**Deep-Tech Matrix — Axis Definitions (cf. HTGF DTM, Berger/Joeressen/Fiebig 2025):**

**Y-Axis: Technology Readiness Level (TRL 1–9)**

| TRL Cluster | TRL | Description |
|-------------|:---:|-------------|
| Scientific Breakthrough | 1–2 | Basic research, observed principles |
| Proof of Concept | 3–4 | Technology concept validated, laboratory validation |
| Demonstration | 5–6 | Validation in relevant / operational environment |
| Engineering Execution | 7–8 | System demonstration / qualified in operational environment |
| Production Ready | 9 | Technology market-ready, series production |

**X-Axis: Market Certainty for a Huge Market (1–6)**

| Level | Label | Description |
|:---:|---|---|
| 1 | Unclear if there is a market at all | No identifiable market application |
| 2 | Niche market | Small, limited market identified |
| 3 | Established niche, first indications of scaling | Niche established, early scaling signals |
| 4 | High momentum, clear indications of scaling | Strong momentum, clear scaling indicators |
| 5 | Mainstream Market (>€10B) | Mainstream market, validated size >€10B |
| 6 | Blockbuster Market (>€100B) | Blockbuster market >€100B |

**Matrix Zones and Investability:**

| Zone | TRL Range | Market Range | Characteristics |
|------|:---:|:---:|---|
| **No Money Land** | TRL 1–3 at Market 1–2 | Low × Low | "Double Bet" — not VC-fundable. Also: TRL >6 without identifiable market ("Technology in search of a problem") |
| **Pre-Seed** | TRL 3–5 | Market 2–4 | Use-case hypothesis with initial technical feasibility and IP. Transition from "Technology Push" to "Application Pull" |
| **Seed** | TRL 4–6 | Market 3–5 | Customer validation through LOIs, pilot projects, PoC. Prototype / demo system functional. Team growing |
| **Series A** | TRL 5–7 | Market 4–6 | Science risk resolved, MVP available, engineering execution. First commercial contracts, credible GTM strategy |
| **Series B** | TRL 7–9 | Market 5–6 | Product ready for scaling. Proof of repeatability, robust GTM strategy, significant revenue traction |

- Determine the current TRL position and Market Certainty position (from 4.3). Record both as a coordinate pair [TRL X / Market Y].
- Check: Which zone does the company occupy? Does the zone match the requested financing round?
- **"No Money Land" check:** Does the company fall in the low TRL AND low Market Certainty area? All surveyed late-stage investors categorically exclude this "double bet."
- **Also identify "technology in search of a problem":** A company with TRL >6 but Market Certainty 1–2 is also in "No Money Land" — even TRL-9 products without an attractive market cannot raise VC capital.
- Plot the progression path (trajectory): Where does the company stand today, where should it stand with this financing round, and which zone should be reached by the next round?
- Compare to portfolio: Do we already have investments in this matrix zone? Does this investment increase concentration risk?

**Deep-Tech Timeline Context:**
- SaaS startups typically scale revenues starting ~24 months after founding.
- Deep-tech startups typically require >48 months to reach revenue scale (physical product, FOAK unit, lab-to-fab transition).
- After 7 years, revenues are comparable — however, the financing taxonomy is shifted by approximately 2 rounds: what qualifies as "Seed" in SaaS corresponds to "Pre-Seed" in deep-tech. What qualifies as "Series A" in SaaS is still "Seed" in deep-tech.
- **Implication for assessment:** Do not compare deep-tech stage labels with SaaS expectations. The Deep-Tech Matrix defines stage-specific expectations explicitly (see 6.4).

> *Red Flag triggers: TRL <4 at Seed without a funded, time-bound development plan. TRL claims not supported by reproducible evidence.*
>
> *Stage note: Seed expects TRL 4–6. Series A expects TRL 6–8.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) TRL level and evidence quality (reproducible data, credible roadmap) and (b) DTM zone investability (avoids double bet, clear trajectory to next zone). Score 5 if TRL meets or exceeds stage threshold with reproducible evidence, occupies a favorable single-risk position with clear trajectory, and development roadmap is credible. Score 3 if TRL is at lower bound of stage expectations or DTM position is near "double bet" boundary but a credible plan exists. Score 0–1 if TRL is below stage threshold, evidence is not reproducible, company is positioned in "No Money Land," or a "double bet" applies.*
>
> *Source: HTGF Deep-Tech Matrix, Berger/Joeressen/Fiebig, 2025.*

### 3.3 — IP & Freedom to Operate [0–5] 🚩 RED FLAG

All IP is exclusively assigned to the company without restriction, the patent portfolio covers the core innovation with appropriate jurisdictional breadth (filed, pending, or granted), and trade secret protection measures are in place. For university spin-offs: IP has been cleanly and fully transferred from the institution without retained rights, reversion clauses, or burdensome royalty obligations. The company has established freedom to operate (FTO) — the ability to commercialize the product without infringing on valid IP rights of third parties. A formal FTO analysis has been conducted by qualified patent counsel, no unresolved blocking patents exist, and the litigation exposure is low. Where blocking patents have been identified, credible design-arounds or licensing strategies are in place.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Patent Portfolio & IP Ownership Assessment:**
- Request a complete IP schedule: List all patents (filed, pending, granted) with filing dates, jurisdictions, and claim scope summaries.
- For each patent: Who is the named inventor? Who is the assignee? If the assignee is not the company, flag immediately — this is a Red Flag.
- For university spin-offs: Request the IP transfer agreement. Check: Is the transfer exclusive? Are there retained rights for the university (e.g., right to use for research, revenue sharing, reversion clauses)? Is there an ongoing royalty obligation?
- Check for trade secret protection: Are NDAs in place with all employees, contractors, and partners who have accessed core technology? Is there a documented trade secret inventory?
- Benchmark: XB100 deep-tech companies average 122 patent documents. A seed-stage company won't match this, but having at least 1–2 filed patents or a concrete filing strategy is expected.
- Ask: "What is your IP strategy for the next 24 months?" — a team without a patent strategy at seed is a concern.

**Freedom to Operate Assessment:**
- Ask: "Has a Freedom to Operate analysis been conducted by qualified patent counsel?" If yes, request the FTO opinion letter and note its date, scope, and conclusions.
- If no FTO analysis exists: Flag this as a gap. For Seed, a preliminary landscape search is the minimum; for Series A, a formal FTO opinion is expected.
- Search patent databases (Google Patents, Espacenet) for the core technology area. Identify the 3–5 most relevant third-party patents and discuss with the founders.
- Assess litigation exposure: Is the company operating in a patent-dense field (e.g., semiconductors, biotech)? Are there known patent trolls active in the space?
- For design-arounds: If blocking patents exist, has the team identified a technical workaround? Has patent counsel confirmed the workaround is outside the claim scope?

> *Red Flag triggers: No own IP. IP still owned by university or employer. Core technology based solely on open-source with no proprietary layer. No FTO analysis conducted. Known blocking patents without license or design-around strategy.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) patent portfolio quality (ownership, assignment, coverage of core innovation) and (b) freedom to operate (FTO analysis, blocking patent risk, litigation exposure). Score 5 if all IP is exclusively assigned, portfolio covers core innovation with appropriate jurisdictional breadth, trade secrets are protected, formal FTO analysis is complete with no unresolved blockers, and litigation exposure is low. Score 2–3 if IP ownership is clean but FTO analysis is preliminary, or if minor blocking patents exist with credible design-arounds. Score 0–1 if IP ownership is contested, no FTO analysis exists, or known blocking patents exist without resolution strategy.*

### 3.4 — Scalability & Readiness [0–5]

The technology architecture supports 10x–100x scale without fundamental redesign. Technical debt is controlled, CI/CD and automated testing are established, and the codebase is documented. For software: infrastructure scales horizontally with manageable dependencies. For hardware: design is modular with multi-source component availability. For HW/SW combos: integration complexity at scale is manageable with independent software update capability. The path from working prototype to series production is credible and well-planned. BOM costs show a realistic cost-down curve from current volume to target scale, a qualified manufacturing partner is identified and contractually engaged (LOI or contract), and all required regulatory certifications (CE, UL, FDA, AI Act) are mapped with timelines and budgets. The team has production experience or has secured manufacturing expertise.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Technical Scalability & Architecture Assessment:**
- For software companies: Request an architecture diagram. Ask CTO to walk through what happens when user count / data volume / transaction rate increases 10x and 100x. Where are the bottlenecks? What needs to be re-architected?
- Apply the Point Nine Capital framework — evaluate two axes: *speed* (ability to iterate and ship quickly) and *reliability* (ability to scale without breaking). Classify as: "Technically Sound," "Workable," or "Here Be Dragons."
- **Technical debt assessment (often overlooked):** Ask: What percentage of engineering time is spent on maintenance vs. new features? Is the codebase documented? Are there automated tests? Is CI/CD in place? Red flags: reliance on contractors for core product, outdated technology stacks, insufficient documentation, no version control discipline.
- For hardware: Is the design modular (can components be swapped / upgraded)? Are critical components available from multiple suppliers? What is the supply chain lead time for key parts?
- For HW/SW integration: How tightly coupled are hardware and software? Can software be updated independently (OTA)? What happens when hardware revisions are needed?

**Industrialization Readiness Assessment (BOM, Production, Certification):**
- Request the current BOM with unit costs at prototype volume (10 units) vs. projected costs at scale (1,000 / 10,000 / 100,000 units). Is the cost-down curve realistic? What specific assumptions drive the cost reduction (volume discounts, design simplification, component substitution)?
- **Techno-Economic Analysis (TEA):** For deep-tech, ask: "What unit economics does the technology enable at current TRL vs. at production TRL?" Key question: What assumptions must hold true for this to work at scale? What has been demonstrated in the lab vs. what is required for series production?
- Manufacturing partner: Is a contract manufacturer identified? Is there a signed LOI or contract, or is it aspirational? Has the partner produced similar products before? Is there a backup manufacturer?
- Certifications: List all required certifications (CE, UL, FDA, AI Act, etc.) with timeline and estimated cost. Has the certification process been started? Are test labs identified?
- Deep-tech capital reality: XB100 companies average $801M raised over 9.2 years. Hardware ventures systematically underestimate R&D-to-production costs by 2–5x. Score harshly if the team has no production experience and no manufacturing partner.

> *Deep-tech reality check: "Hardware is hard." R&D costs to series readiness are systematically underestimated. Score harshly if the team lacks production experience and has no manufacturing partner.*
>
> *Stage note: At Seed, a credible industrialization plan with identified partners is sufficient. At Series A, BOM cost-down evidence and manufacturing LOIs are expected.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) software/system scalability (architecture, tech debt, CI/CD) and (b) hardware industrialization readiness (BOM cost-down, manufacturing partners, certification roadmap). Weight (b) higher for hardware-centric deep-tech; weight (a) higher for software-centric. Score 5 if architecture supports 10x–100x scale without redesign, technical debt is controlled, CI/CD is established, BOM shows credible cost-down path, manufacturing partner is contractually engaged, and certifications are mapped with timelines. Score 2–3 if architecture is workable but has known bottlenecks, or if industrialization plan is credible but partners are not yet contractually committed. Score 0–1 if fundamental redesign is needed for scale, technical debt is uncontrolled, or no credible path to production exists.*

### 3.5 — External Validation [0–5]

The core technology is independently validated through external technical audits, peer-reviewed publications in top-tier venues, or CTO-level endorsement from pilot customers. Results have been reproduced outside the founder's lab, and the team's technical credibility is recognized through industry awards, accelerator admissions, or invited conference presentations.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Has an independent technical expert (not affiliated with the company) reviewed the core technology? Request the report or arrange a call.
- List peer-reviewed publications by the team in the relevant technical domain. Are they published in top-tier journals/conferences for the field?
- Have pilot customers conducted their own technical evaluation? Can you speak to a CTO or technical lead at a pilot customer to confirm the technology works as claimed?
- For deep-tech: Have the results been independently reproduced outside the founder's lab? Lab results that only work in one specific setup are a concern.
- Industry recognition: Awards, accelerator admissions (e.g., EIC Accelerator, Y Combinator, Techstars), or invitations to speak at top industry events.

### 3.6 — Obsolescence Risk [0–5]

The company's core technological advantage is durable and resilient against external paradigm shifts within the 5–7 year investment horizon. The moat widens over time (through accumulating proprietary data, progressive IP, or network effects) rather than narrowing. Platform absorption risk is low, and the technology is architecturally flexible enough to adapt if the landscape shifts. This assessment evaluates structural technology evolution risk and is separate from competitive replication risk (covered in 5.4).

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Map the technology against the current state of the art: What is the dominant research direction in this field? Could a breakthrough in an adjacent field (e.g., foundation models for robotics, new battery chemistry, quantum computing for optimization) render this approach obsolete?
- Ask the founders: "What technology development would most threaten your approach? How would you respond?" Founders who can articulate this risk clearly and have a contingency plan score higher.
- **Platform absorption risk:** Could a large platform (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure, Siemens MindSphere) build this as a feature of their existing platform? For AI/ML companies: Could the next generation of foundation models commoditize your proprietary capability?
- **Architectural risk:** Is the technology built on a stack or standard that could be superseded? (e.g., a specific communication protocol, a hardware interface, a data format). Is the architecture flexible enough to adapt to paradigm shifts?
- Assess the technology's "moat decay rate": Is the advantage widening over time (e.g., through accumulating proprietary data, network effects, or progressive IP) or narrowing (e.g., as open-source alternatives improve or costs drop)?

| Obsolescence Risk | Time Horizon | Score Guidance |
|---|---|:---:|
| No foreseeable paradigm shift; advantage widens with time | >10 years | 5 |
| Known emerging alternatives but 5+ year transition period | 5–10 years | 4 |
| Active research could produce alternatives within investment horizon | 3–5 years | 3 |
| Adjacent technology advancing rapidly; pivot may be needed | 2–3 years | 2 |
| Platform absorption or open-source commoditization imminent | <2 years | 0–1 |

> *Key question for the IC: "If we invest today and the technology landscape shifts in 3 years, can this team pivot — or are they locked into an approach that could become stranded?"*

**Chapter 3 — Red Flags (automatic Pass triggers):**
- 🚩 Technology assessed as "Feature, not Product" — incremental improvement without defensible moat
- 🚩 TRL <4 at Seed without funded development plan
- 🚩 IP not owned by the company (university, employer, or co-founder holdback)
- 🚩 No FTO analysis; known blocking patents without resolution strategy
- 🚩 BOM not scalable and no credible production cost-down path (hardware ventures)
- 🚩 Core technology facing imminent obsolescence (<2 years) from platform absorption, open-source commoditization, or paradigm shift with no pivot capability
