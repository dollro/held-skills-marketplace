### Technology Category Classification

Before applying the financial stage corridors, classify the company into one of three technology categories. This classification determines which benchmark corridor to use throughout this chapter. The distinction matters because capital intensity, time-to-revenue, grant eligibility, and investor expectations differ fundamentally across categories.

| Category | Definition | Examples | Key Financial Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Deep-Tech** | Novel science or engineering at the core; proprietary hardware, materials, or processes requiring significant R&D and industrialization. TRL journey typically starts ≤4. | Novel sensors, new materials, biotech, quantum, fusion, custom robotics, novel chip design | Highest capital intensity; longest time-to-revenue; highest grant eligibility; revenue often project-based before recurring; gross margins variable (40–70%) depending on HW/SW mix |
| **Shallow-Tech (Applied-Tech)** | Software-driven innovation deployed on commercially available, off-the-shelf hardware. The core IP is in the software/algorithms/AI, not in the hardware itself. May include system integration. | AI control software on standard robots, IoT platforms on commercial sensors, computer vision on standard cameras, SaaS + commodity HW bundles | Moderate capital intensity; HW is pass-through cost (compresses gross margins vs. pure SW); faster to first revenue than deep-tech; moderate grant eligibility; path to recurring revenue via SaaS/usage-based models |
| **Pure Software** | No hardware component; cloud-delivered or on-premise software. Value creation entirely in code, data, or algorithms. | Enterprise SaaS, developer tools, data analytics platforms, AI/ML services (API-delivered), marketplaces | Lowest capital intensity; fastest time-to-revenue; lowest grant eligibility (fewer R&D programs apply); highest gross margins (>70%); clearest path to recurring revenue |

**How to classify (junior analyst checklist):**
- Ask: "If I remove the hardware, does the core value proposition still exist?" If yes → Pure SW or Shallow-Tech. If no → Deep-Tech.
- Ask: "Does the company manufacture or design proprietary hardware?" If yes → Deep-Tech. If it uses off-the-shelf/commercially available hardware → Shallow-Tech.
- Ask: "Is the primary delivery mechanism a software license, SaaS subscription, or API?" If yes with no hardware → Pure SW. If yes bundled with hardware → Shallow-Tech.
- **Borderline cases:** Some companies straddle categories (e.g., a company developing novel gripper hardware + AI control software). In such cases, classify based on where the *primary R&D risk and capital requirement* sits. If >60% of R&D spend goes to hardware/materials/manufacturing, classify as Deep-Tech. If >60% goes to software/algorithms, classify as Shallow-Tech.

> *Document the classification and rationale in the analyst notes. If a company is borderline, note which corridor you're using and why. IC members may challenge the classification — being explicit about the reasoning prevents ambiguity.*

### 7.1 — Burn Rate, Runway & Capital Efficiency [0–5] 🚩 RED FLAG

The monthly burn rate results in a runway of at least 18 months post-close, with sufficient buffer (≥3 months) between the expected milestone date and cash-out date. The company converts capital into traction efficiently — measured by cost-per-milestone (primary at Pre-Seed/Seed) or burn multiple (primary once revenue is recurring) — with an improving capital efficiency trend even as operations scale.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Burn Rate & Runway Assessment:**
- Request last 6 months of bank statements or cash flow statements. Calculate average monthly net burn (total cash out minus total cash in).
- Calculate runway: (Current cash + expected investment proceeds) ÷ monthly net burn = months of runway. Minimum 18 months post-close.
- Map runway against the milestone plan (7.2): Is there at least 3 months of buffer between the expected milestone date and cash-out date? If not, the company is fundraising under pressure.
- Ask: "What is your burn rate trajectory? When does it peak? When does it start declining?" Deep-tech and Shallow-Tech companies often have increasing burn as they approach production or first deployments — model this explicitly. Pure SW companies should show a more predictable burn trajectory.

**Capital Efficiency Assessment — Stage-Appropriate Metric Selection:**

At Pre-Seed and Seed, most companies (especially Deep-Tech and Shallow-Tech) have zero or negligible recurring revenue, which makes the burn multiple formula (net burn ÷ net new ARR) produce undefined or misleading results. Use the appropriate primary metric for the company's stage and technology category:

**(A) Pre-Revenue / Pre-Seed / Seed: Cost-per-Milestone (Primary Metric)**

Calculate the total capital consumed to date and map it against the milestones achieved. This reveals whether the team is deploying capital efficiently relative to comparable companies. Benchmark ranges vary significantly by technology category:

| Milestone | Deep-Tech Benchmark | Shallow-Tech Benchmark | Pure SW Benchmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| First working prototype / demo | €500K–2M | €200K–800K | €100K–400K |
| First patent filed/granted | €50–150K (legal costs within R&D) | €30–100K (if applicable) | Rarely applicable |
| First paid pilot / customer | €800K–2M total spend | €300K–1M total spend | €150K–500K total spend |
| TRL 4→5 advancement | €500K–2M | €200K–600K | n/a (no TRL framework) |
| MVP with repeatable deployment | €1.5–4M | €500K–1.5M | €200K–600K |

How to calculate: Sum all cash spent (from founding or from last round close) and divide by the milestones achieved. Compare to what peer companies in the same technology category, sector, and geography spent to reach equivalent milestones. Sources for benchmarks: comparable deep-tech portfolio data, EIF reports, Dealroom sector analyses, and direct IC experience.

Ask founders: "How much total capital have you consumed to get to where you are today, and what are the three most important things that capital bought?" The quality of the answer reveals capital discipline.

**(B) Revenue-Generating Companies: Burn Multiple (Primary once ARR > €100K)**

| Burn Multiple | Rating | Notes |
|:---:|---|---|
| <1.5x | Excellent | Rare at Seed; common signal of capital-efficient Pure SW |
| 1.5–2.5x | Good | Target range for Shallow-Tech and Pure SW at Series A |
| 2.5–5x | Acceptable | Normal for Deep-Tech at Series A; concerning for Pure SW |
| >5x | Concerning | Acceptable only at early Seed for Deep-Tech with clear improving trend |

Calculate: Total net cash burned in last 12 months ÷ net new ARR added in same period. For companies transitioning from pre-revenue to early revenue, calculate both metrics and note the trajectory — cost-per-milestone should be declining and burn multiple should be improving quarter over quarter.

> *Technology category matters here: A burn multiple of 3x is unremarkable for a Deep-Tech company deploying its first hardware installations, but it is a warning sign for a Pure SW company at the same revenue level. Always compare against the correct benchmark set.*

**(C) General Efficiency Indicators (All Stages):**
- Is capital efficiency improving over time (more output per € spent) or deteriorating?
- Capital intensity context by category:
  - Deep-Tech: XB100 companies average $801M raised over 9.2 years. High capital intensity is structural, not a red flag per se — but discipline within that envelope is critical.
  - Shallow-Tech: Moderate capital intensity. The hardware is purchased, not developed — so R&D burn should be predominantly software engineering and integration, not materials science or manufacturing.
  - Pure SW: Low capital intensity. A Pure SW company burning >€150K/month at Seed without corresponding ARR growth is a concern.
- Compare headcount efficiency: Revenue (or pipeline value) per employee vs. sector benchmarks.

| Metric | Deep-Tech Benchmark | Shallow-Tech Benchmark | Pure SW Benchmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue per employee (at scale) | €100–200K | €150–250K | €200–400K |
| Typical team size at Seed | 10–25 | 8–18 | 5–12 |
| Monthly burn at Seed | €80–250K | €50–150K | €30–100K |

> *Red Flag triggers: Runway <12 months post-close without clear bridge financing plan. Burn rate increasing faster than traction. Burn multiple >5x with no improving trend. At Seed: total capital consumed exceeds 2× the technology-category benchmark with no corresponding milestone outperformance.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) runway adequacy (≥18 months post-close with ≥3 months buffer to milestone) and (b) capital efficiency (stage- and category-appropriate metric within acceptable range, improving trend). Score 5 if runway exceeds 18 months with ample buffer and capital efficiency is demonstrably strong vs. category benchmark. Score 2–3 if runway is adequate but capital efficiency is average or not yet measurable. Score 0–1 if runway is <12 months without a bridge plan, or if capital efficiency is poor with a deteriorating trend.*

### 7.2 — Use of Proceeds & Non-Dilutive Funding [0–5]

The capital deployment plan is tied to specific, measurable milestones that de-risk the business: each budget line (R&D → TRL advancement or product milestones, Sales → customer acquisition targets, Ops → production or deployment readiness) has a corresponding concrete deliverable with a timeline. The company proactively pursues a non-dilutive funding strategy appropriate to its technology category, with grants already secured or applications in progress, and the public-to-total funding ratio aligns with stage benchmarks.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Milestone Alignment Assessment:**
- Request the use-of-proceeds breakdown by category (R&D, Sales & Marketing, G&A, Production, Hiring). For each category, identify the specific milestone it funds and the timeline.
- Challenge each budget line: "If you spend €X on R&D, what specifically will be delivered by when?" The answer should be a concrete deliverable (e.g., "TRL 6 demonstration with pilot customer data by Q3 2027" for Deep-Tech; "10 production deployments and €300K ARR by Q2 2027" for Shallow-Tech; "€500K ARR with <18 month CAC payback by Q4 2026" for Pure SW), not a vague activity ("continued product development").
- Cross-reference with runway (7.1): Does the total budget match the runway calculation? Are there unfunded gaps?
- Check for capital efficiency: Are there areas where the same milestone could be achieved with fewer resources? Are founder salaries reasonable?

**Non-Dilutive Funding Strategy Assessment:**

The relevance and availability of non-dilutive funding varies significantly by technology category. Deep-Tech companies have the broadest grant eligibility and should have the highest public funding ratios. Pure SW companies have fewer applicable programs but should still pursue available instruments (R&D tax credits, innovation prizes, accelerator grants).

- Ask: "What non-dilutive funding have you received, applied for, or identified?" Document:

| Non-Dilutive Source | Typical Category Fit | Status | Amount | Timeline | Notes |
|---|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|---|
| EIC Accelerator (grants up to €2.5M + equity) | Deep-Tech, some Shallow-Tech | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| EXIST Transfer / EXIST Startup Grant | All (university spinoffs) | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| ZIM (Zentrales Innovationsprogramm Mittelstand) | Deep-Tech, Shallow-Tech | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| Horizon Europe / EIC Pathfinder | Deep-Tech | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| KfW programs / Wachstumsfonds Deutschland | All | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| Regional / Bundesland programs (e.g., BayTOU, NRW.BANK) | All (regional eligibility) | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| R&D tax credit (Forschungszulage) | All (most accessible for Pure SW) | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| INVEST Zuschuss für Wagniskapital (angel investor grant) | All (investor-facing) | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |
| Other (industry grants, foundation funding, prizes) | Varies | [Received / Applied / Identified / N/A] | [€ ___] | [Date] | |

- Score higher if the company has a proactive grant strategy with applications in progress and grants already secured. Score lower if the company has not explored non-dilutive funding despite eligibility.
- **Integration with financing plan:** Does the financial model incorporate expected grant income? Are grant timelines aligned with cash needs? Is there a dedicated person or advisor managing grant applications?

**Public Funding Ratio — Stage & Category Benchmark Check:**

Calculate the company's public-to-total funding ratio and compare against the benchmark for its technology category and target stage.

| Metric | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Total non-dilutive funding received to date | Sum of all grants, subsidies, tax credits, prizes | € ___ |
| Total funding received to date (incl. this round) | Non-dilutive + all equity rounds + convertibles | € ___ |
| **Public Funding Ratio** | **Non-dilutive ÷ Total funding × 100** | **____%** |

| Stage | Deep-Tech Benchmark | Shallow-Tech Benchmark | Pure SW Benchmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-Seed | 50% | 30–40% | 10–25% |
| Seed | 50% | 25–40% | 10–20% |
| Series A | 25% | 15–25% | 5–10% |
| Series B | 10% | 5–10% | <5% |

**Gap assessment:** [Above / On target / Below / Significantly below] vs. category-appropriate benchmark.

How to interpret:
- **Above or on target:** Positive signal — the team is leveraging the European funding ecosystem effectively, reducing dilution and extending runway. Verify that grant conditions do not constrain commercial freedom (some EU grants restrict licensing or geographic focus).
- **Moderately below (within 15pp of category benchmark):** Acceptable if the company has a credible plan to close the gap (applications in progress) or operates in a sector with limited grant eligibility.
- **Significantly below (>15pp gap) with no explanation:** Flag as a missed opportunity. Ask: "Why have you not pursued [specific eligible program]?" Common valid reasons include timing constraints, eligibility mismatches, or strategic decisions to avoid grant-related IP constraints. Common *invalid* reasons include lack of awareness (signals weak strategic planning) or "we don't need grants" (signals capital indiscipline).
- **Pure SW significantly below:** Less concerning than for Deep-Tech. Pure SW companies have legitimately fewer grant options and faster paths to revenue that reduce dependency on non-dilutive funding. However, the Forschungszulage (R&D tax credit) is available to virtually all qualifying German companies — not claiming it is simply leaving money on the table.

> *Deep-tech benchmark: 29% of XB100 companies received government funding, rising to 49% for space, agriculture, climate, and quantum sectors. A Deep-Tech company without a non-dilutive funding strategy is leaving money on the table. For Pure SW, the bar is lower — but the Forschungszulage alone can be worth €500K–1M and should always be pursued.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) milestone alignment quality (specific deliverables, realistic timelines, complete budget coverage) and (b) non-dilutive funding strategy (grants secured or in progress, public funding ratio aligned with category-appropriate benchmarks). Score 5 if each budget line is tied to specific milestones with realistic timelines and the public funding ratio meets the category benchmark with grants secured. Score 2–3 if milestone alignment is adequate but non-dilutive funding has not been fully explored, or vice versa. Score 0–1 if budget lines are vague activities without deliverables and no non-dilutive funding strategy exists despite clear eligibility.*

### 7.3 — Financial Model & Scenario Robustness [0–5]

Revenue projections are grounded in bottom-up assumptions (named customers × ACV × conversion probability × timing) rather than top-down market share claims. Cost assumptions are realistic and benchmarked, growth rates are validated against comparable companies at similar stages, and key assumptions are explicitly stated and stress-testable. A 3-scenario model (bull/base/bear) demonstrates that in the bear case, the company still reaches a fundable milestone before cash-out. Revenue projections are sanity-checked against the stage corridors for the company's technology category to catch obviously disconnected financial plans.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Financial Model Plausibility Assessment:**
- Request the financial model (Excel). Check: Are revenue projections bottom-up (named customers × ACV × conversion probability × timing) or top-down ("we'll capture 2% of a $10B market")?
- Identify the 3–5 key assumptions that drive the model (e.g., conversion rate, ACV, churn rate, hiring pace, BOM cost). For each: What is the evidence? What happens if the assumption is 50% wrong?
- Benchmark growth rates against the company's technology category:

| Growth Rate Benchmark | Deep-Tech | Shallow-Tech | Pure SW |
|---|---|---|---|
| Typical YoY revenue growth (Seed → Series A) | 1.5–3× | 2–4× | 2.5–5× (T2D3 pattern) |
| Typical YoY revenue growth (Series A → B) | 2–3× | 2–3× | 2–3× |
| Time from first revenue to €1M ARR | 24–48 months | 18–30 months | 12–24 months |
| Time from €1M ARR to €5M ARR | 18–36 months | 12–24 months | 12–18 months |

> *Note: The "T2D3" pattern (triple, triple, double, double, double) is a Pure SW/SaaS benchmark. Applying T2D3 expectations to Deep-Tech or Shallow-Tech companies will produce unrealistic projections. If a Deep-Tech founder presents T2D3 growth, challenge with: "What comparable deep-tech company has achieved this growth rate? What was their ARR base when they started tripling?"*

- Check cost realism: Are salaries benchmarked to market? Is office/infrastructure cost included? Are R&D costs realistic for the technology category?

| Cost Realism Check | Deep-Tech | Shallow-Tech | Pure SW |
|---|---|---|---|
| R&D as % of total spend (Seed) | 50–70% | 40–60% | 50–70% |
| COGS / gross margin trajectory | Improving from 30–50% → >50% | Improving from 45–55% → >60% | >65% from early revenue, → >75% |
| HW BOM / manufacturing costs | Significant, requires cost-down plan | Moderate (off-the-shelf), largely pass-through | None |
| Common underestimation | Manufacturing scale-up, certification | Integration/deployment labor, support costs | Customer success headcount, infrastructure |

**Revenue Corridor Sanity Check:**

This is a fast diagnostic to catch financial plans that are disconnected from stage-appropriate expectations. Map the company's projected annual revenue against the corridor for its technology category at each stage it expects to traverse:

| Year | Projected Revenue | Implied Stage (by revenue, using category corridor) | Planned Stage (by round) | Alignment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Year 0 (current) | € ___ | [Pre-Seed / Seed / Series A / Series B] | [___] | [✓ / ⚠ / ✗] |
| Year 1 | € ___ | [___] | [___] | [✓ / ⚠ / ✗] |
| Year 2 | € ___ | [___] | [___] | [✓ / ⚠ / ✗] |
| Year 3 | € ___ | [___] | [___] | [✓ / ⚠ / ✗] |
| Year 4 | € ___ | [___] | [___] | [✓ / ⚠ / ✗] |

How to fill in: For each year, look up the projected revenue in the founder's financial model. Then determine which stage corridor that revenue figure falls into *using the correct technology category table* (A, B, or C from the reference tables above). Compare against which stage/round the company expects to be at in that year per their fundraising roadmap.

How to interpret:
- **Aligned (✓):** Revenue projections are consistent with the stage the company expects to be at. This does not mean the projections are correct — only that they are internally coherent with the fundraising plan.
- **Ahead (⚠ — revenue outpaces stage):** The model projects revenue that implies a *later* stage than the company plans to fundraise at. This is the most common pattern in startup financial plans and signals overoptimism. Flag any year where projected revenue exceeds the *next* stage's upper bound without a corresponding round having occurred. Example: A Seed-stage Deep-Tech company projecting >€5M revenue (Series B corridor) by Year 3 without having raised Series A is presenting an implausible timeline — this pattern is empirically almost non-existent.
- **Behind (⚠ — revenue lags stage):** The model projects revenue that implies an *earlier* stage than the company plans to fundraise at. This may signal realistic conservatism (positive) or an inability to articulate a credible revenue path (negative). Cross-reference with the scenario analysis below to determine which.

> *Critical category nuance: A Pure SW company at Seed projecting €3M ARR in Year 2 is ambitious but within the realm of possibility (many SaaS companies achieve this). The same projection from a Deep-Tech company at Seed is almost certainly fantasy — it implies achieving Series B-level revenue within the Seed funding period, which contradicts the empirical base rate for >200 deep-tech companies in the Deep-Tech Matrix dataset (Berger/Joeressen/Fiebig, 2025).*
>
> *This is a reality check, not a forecasting tool. The corridors reflect where companies in each category actually were at each stage — not where founders thought they would be. Use this to calibrate founder projections against the empirical base rate for their technology category.*

**Scenario Stress Test Assessment:**

Build (or request the founders to build) a 3-scenario model. This turns qualitative risks into quantified financial impact:

| Scenario | Revenue Assumption | Cost Assumption | Cash-Out Date | Key Milestone Impact |
|---|---|---|:---:|---|
| **Bull case** | 80% of founder target + 20% optimistic | Costs on plan | [Date] | [Milestones achieved ahead of schedule] |
| **Base case** | Founder's base projection | Costs on plan + 10% buffer | [Date] | [Milestones achieved on time] |
| **Bear case** | 75% of founder's worst case | Costs +20% over plan | [Date] | [Which milestones are at risk?] |

- The critical output: **In the bear case, does the company still have enough runway to reach a fundable milestone before cash-out?** If not, what is the bridge financing plan?
- Category-specific stress scenarios:
  - **Deep-Tech:** Add a "technical delay" scenario — what happens if TRL advancement takes 6–12 months longer than planned? This is the most common deep-tech failure mode.
  - **Shallow-Tech:** Add a "deployment friction" scenario — what happens if customer deployments take 2× longer than planned due to integration complexity, IT procurement delays, or change management resistance?
  - **Pure SW:** Add a "sales cycle extension" scenario — what happens if the sales cycle is 50% longer than modeled and churn is 2× the assumption?
- Stress-test runway (cross-reference 7.1): What if burn increases 20% (e.g., hiring accelerates) and revenue comes 6 months later than planned? Does runway still cover the next fundraise milestone?

> *Reality check: At Seed, the financial model is a structured hypothesis, not a forecast. Score the quality of assumptions and internal consistency — not the topline number. A team that presents only a bull case without acknowledging downside scenarios scores lower on both financial acumen and transparency.*
>
> *Best practice (Sequoia / DoorDash memo): Explicit "If everything goes right…" and "If things don't go right…" scenarios with specific risk enumeration.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) revenue model quality (bottom-up grounding, realistic assumptions, category-appropriate corridor alignment) and (b) scenario robustness (bear case still fundable, category-specific stress tests conducted). Score 5 if revenue projections are rigorously bottom-up, pass the corridor sanity check for the correct technology category, and the bear case remains fundable. Score 2–3 if the model has some bottom-up elements but key assumptions are unverified, or the corridor check shows moderate misalignment. Score 0–1 if the model is purely top-down fantasy, shows severe corridor disconnects for the technology category, or the bear case shows cash-out before milestones.*

### 7.4 — Path to Next Round [0–5]

A credible bridge exists from this round to the next financing event: the milestones required for the next round are achievable with this round's capital. The deal's financial parameters — round volume, pre-money valuation, and revenue — fit within the stage corridors for the company's technology category, and the valuation trajectory allows a realistic step-up at the next round. Co-investors are positioned for pro-rata follow-on with confirmed reserves.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Milestone-to-Next-Round Bridge:**
- Define the "must-hit" milestones for the next round: What does a typical next-stage investor in this sector and technology category require? Map against the company's roadmap — can they get there with this round's capital?
- For the non-financial dimensions (Tech, Market, Commercial, Team readiness for next stage), cross-reference the assessments from Chapters 1–6 and the stage readiness diagnostic in Backup Slide B. Chapter 7 does not re-score these but should flag any dimension where Chapters 1–6 identified a gap that would prevent successful follow-on fundraising.
- Evaluate the fundraising narrative: At the planned milestone achievement, what is the story for the next round? Is it compelling enough to attract next-stage investors?

**Financial Parameter Fit — Category-Appropriate Corridor Check:**

Check whether the deal's financial parameters fall within the stage corridors for the company's technology category (reference tables at chapter opening). This determines whether the deal is appropriately "sized" for its stage.

| Parameter | This Deal | Category Corridor (target stage) | Fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Round Volume | € ___ | [corridor for category + target stage] | [Within / Below / Above] |
| Pre-Money Valuation | € ___ | [corridor for category + target stage] | [Within / Below / Above] |
| Pre-Money as × of Post-Money | ___× | [see category table] | [Within / Below / Above] |
| Current Revenue + Orders | € ___ | [corridor for category + target stage] | [Within / Below / Above] |

How to interpret:
- **All within corridor:** Deal is stage-appropriate for its technology category. No adjustment needed.
- **Round volume below corridor:** May signal either capital efficiency (positive — the team can achieve milestones with less capital) or inability to attract sufficient investor interest (negative). Investigate: Is the smaller round a deliberate choice or a constraint? Does the reduced capital still fund the milestones required for the next round?
- **Round volume above corridor:** May signal either ambition (positive — the team is raising to accelerate) or premature scaling (negative). Investigate: Can the team productively deploy the larger amount, or will excess capital create bloat?
- **Pre-money above corridor:** Valuation may be pricing in future success, leaving insufficient room for step-up at the next round. Calculate the implied step-up required (see below). A step-up requiring >3× in <24 months is aggressive for Deep-Tech; it is ambitious but achievable for Pure SW with strong ARR growth.
- **Pre-money below corridor:** Potential value opportunity. But investigate: Is the low valuation a reflection of genuine early-stage risk, or a sign that more sophisticated investors have passed?
- **Revenue below corridor:** Acceptable at Pre-Seed/Seed for Deep-Tech and Shallow-Tech (where €0 revenue is normal). At Series A, revenue below the corridor floor for the company's category suggests it may not be ready for the stage it's pricing at. Cross-reference with commercial traction assessment (Ch. 6).
- **Revenue above corridor:** Positive signal — commercial traction ahead of stage expectations. Verify that revenue is recurring/repeatable, not one-off project income.

> *Category-mismatch alert: A common valuation error is pricing a Shallow-Tech or Deep-Tech company at Pure SW multiples because "the core IP is software." The corridors differ for a reason: hardware pass-through, longer sales cycles, lower gross margins, and higher deployment costs structurally compress valuations relative to pure SaaS. If the pre-money valuation is within the Pure SW corridor but above the Shallow-Tech corridor, and the company has a hardware component, the deal may be overpriced for its actual margin and scaling profile.*

**Valuation Step-Up Trajectory:**
- Calculate the implied step-up from current round to next round. Use the category-appropriate corridor midpoints as a reference:

| Variable | Calculation | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Current post-money valuation | Pre-money + round size | € ___ |
| Target next-round pre-money (category corridor midpoint) | [See category table] | € ___ |
| **Required step-up multiple** | **Next pre-money ÷ Current post-money** | **___×** |

| Typical step-up by category | Deep-Tech | Shallow-Tech | Pure SW |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seed → Series A | 2–3× | 2–4× | 3–5× |
| Series A → Series B | 2–3× | 2–3× | 2–4× |
| Time between rounds | 18–36 months | 15–24 months | 12–18 months |

| **Assessment** | | **[Achievable / Stretch / Heroic]** |

> *The shorter inter-round period for Pure SW means the same step-up multiple is achieved in less time, which requires faster traction growth. A 3× step-up in 12 months (Pure SW pace) requires very different growth than a 3× step-up in 30 months (Deep-Tech pace). Compare the required annual traction growth to the category benchmarks in 7.3.*

**Co-Investor & Follow-On Assessment:**
- Assess co-investor follow-on capacity (cross-reference 8.3): Do existing investors have reserves for the next round? Will they participate pro-rata? Lack of follow-on capacity from current investors is a negative signal to new investors.
- **Long development cycle risk (Deep-Tech and Shallow-Tech specific):** These companies may need 2–3 funding rounds before generating meaningful recurring revenue. Is the financing plan structured around technical or deployment milestones (tranche-based)? Have bridge financing instruments been discussed in case milestones are delayed?
- **Deep-tech timeline consideration:** "Series A" in Deep-Tech is not "Series A" in SaaS. Multiple late-stage investors view Series A in Deep-Tech as the "second round" — comparable to a SaaS Seed. This means that Deep-Tech Seed companies should benchmark their next-round expectations against what SaaS investors would consider Pre-Seed/Seed metrics, adjusted for the higher capital intensity and longer timelines inherent to hardware and science-based businesses. Shallow-Tech companies sit in between — they benefit from some Deep-Tech patience on timelines but are expected to show commercial traction closer to Pure SW pacing.

> *Fund-specific consideration: Does the plan account for the fund's follow-on capacity and reserves per company, as well as external co-investor syndication?*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) milestone-to-next-round bridge credibility (milestones achievable with this capital, narrative compelling) and (b) financial parameter fit (round volume, valuation, revenue within category-appropriate corridors with realistic step-up trajectory). Score 5 if all financial parameters fit within category corridors, the step-up trajectory is achievable, and milestones clearly bridge to the next round. Score 2–3 if financial parameters are mostly within corridors but the step-up is stretching, or if milestone achievement is execution-dependent with moderate risk. Score 0–1 if the deal is significantly mis-staged (financial parameters outside category corridor in multiple dimensions) or the milestones required for the next round are unachievable with this capital.*

### 7.5 — Exit Potential [0–5]

The exit thesis is credible: 3–5 plausible strategic acquirers can be named with demonstrated M&A appetite in the sector, comparable exits confirm achievable revenue multiples, and the 10x return check (working backwards from proposed valuation and ownership percentage) yields a required exit enterprise value that corresponds to an ARR target realistically achievable within 5–7 years. The expected exit pathway and timeline should be calibrated to the company's technology category.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Name 3–5 plausible strategic acquirers. For each: Why would they want to acquire this company? Have they made acquisitions in this space before? At what multiples?
- Research comparable exits: Search Crunchbase, PitchBook, and press for M&A transactions and IPOs in the sector over the past 3–5 years. Document:

### 7.6 — Financial Hygiene & Reporting [0–5]

Financial reporting is professional and reliable: GAAP/HGB-compliant bookkeeping is performed by a qualified Steuerberater or external accountant, the cap table is clean and matches the Handelsregister, tax compliance (VAT, payroll) is confirmed, and no undisclosed liabilities or off-balance-sheet obligations exist. The team commits to quarterly financial reporting and monthly KPI dashboards (KfW standard).

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Request: Last 12 months of profit & loss, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. If the company cannot produce these, that is itself a finding (negative).
- Check: Is bookkeeping done by a professional (Steuerberater / external accountant) or DIY? For German GmbHs: Is HGB-compliant accounting in place?
- Verify tax compliance: Are VAT filings current? Are payroll taxes current? Any outstanding tax liabilities? (Request a Steuerberater confirmation.)
- Cap table documentation: Is there a clean, current cap table that matches the commercial register (Handelsregister)? For German companies: Are all share transfers notarized? Is the shareholder list (Gesellschafterliste) current?
- Assess reporting willingness: Will the team commit to quarterly financial reporting, monthly KPI dashboards, and board updates? Reluctance to commit is a warning sign.
- Check for undisclosed liabilities: Ask explicitly: "Are there any outstanding loans, guarantees, contingent liabilities, or off-balance-sheet obligations?" Cross-reference with the balance sheet.

>
> *Scoring guidance: Score 5 if all hygiene checks pass and the team proactively commits to investor-grade reporting. Score 3–4 if minor issues exist that are correctable pre-close (e.g., Gesellschafterliste slightly outdated but in process). Score 0–2 if material hygiene failures exist (tax non-compliance, undisclosed liabilities, cap table discrepancies). This criterion is technology-category-agnostic — financial hygiene standards are the same regardless of whether the company is Deep-Tech, Shallow-Tech, or Pure SW.*

**Chapter 7 — Red Flags (automatic Pass triggers):**
- 🚩 Runway <12 months with no bridge financing plan or co-investor commitment
- 🚩 No realistic exit path (no identifiable strategic acquirers, sector too niche for IPO)
- 🚩 Unrealistic financial model with no bottom-up grounding (pure top-down fantasy) or severe corridor disconnects for the company's technology category
- 🚩 Capital-intensive Deep-Tech or Shallow-Tech business without bridge financing plan for production ramp or deployment scale-up
- 🚩 Material financial hygiene failures (undisclosed liabilities, tax non-compliance, cap table irregularities)
- 🚩 Technology category misclassification used to justify inflated valuation (e.g., Shallow-Tech priced at Pure SW multiples without Pure SW margins)
