## 4. Market & Timing

> **Assessment Pillar:** Business Model (market side) | **Weight:** Seed 15% / Series A 15%
>
> The market chapter answers two questions: "Is this market big enough for a venture-scale outcome?" and "Why now — what structural shift makes this the right moment?" The fund targets sustainable market opportunities and positions its investments using the Deep-Tech Matrix's market certainty axis.

### 4.1 — Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM) [0–5]

The TAM is rigorously sized through both top-down and bottom-up approaches, supported by high-quality sources (third-party research, industry reports, government data) with internal consistency. The TAM is large enough to support a 10x+ fund-returning outcome (benchmark: >€1B), and the analysis focuses on the actually reachable market within the company's technology and go-to-market constraints rather than inflated headline figures. The SAM and SOM are credibly derived bottom-up from concrete segmentation by geography, use case, and sales capacity, with realistic market capture assumptions grounded in actual pipeline data, pilot conversion rates, or comparable go-to-market benchmarks. The SOM does not assume >5% market capture in Year 1 without strong supporting evidence.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**TAM Assessment:**
- Request the founder's TAM analysis. Check: Is it top-down only ("the global market for X is $50B") or does it include a bottom-up build (number of target customers × average contract value × purchase frequency)?
- Independently verify: Find 2–3 third-party sources (Gartner, IDC, Statista, industry associations, government statistics) that either confirm or contradict the TAM claim. Note discrepancies.
- Apply the "reality filter": What portion of the stated TAM is actually addressable given the company's technology, geography, price point, and sales capacity? This is the SAM (see below).
- Run the 10x return math backwards: At our proposed ownership %, what exit value is needed for a 10x return? At typical sector multiples (see 6.5), what revenue implies that exit? Is that revenue achievable within the stated TAM?

**SAM/SOM Assessment:**
- SAM = TAM filtered by geography, segment, use case, and technology fit. Ask: "Of the total market, which customers can you actually reach and serve in the next 3–5 years?" This should be a concrete number with named segments.
- SOM = SAM filtered by competitive dynamics and sales capacity. Ask: "How many customers can you realistically acquire in Year 1–3 given your current team and GTM approach?"
- Stress-test: If SOM assumes >5% market capture in Year 1, challenge this — most startups capture <1% in their first year.
- Cross-reference with pipeline (6.1): Does the SOM match the actual customer pipeline and conversion rates?

> *Benchmark: For a seed investment targeting 10x return, the TAM should credibly exceed €1B. SAM should be >€200M.*
>
> *Red Flag indicator: TAM presented without SAM/SOM breakdown. SOM projections assume >5% market capture in Year 1 without evidence.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) TAM size and source quality (top-down/bottom-up convergence, third-party validation, sufficient for 10x return) and (b) SAM/SOM derivation logic (segment-specific, realistic capture assumptions, aligned with pipeline). Score 5 if TAM >€1B with high-quality sources, SAM/SOM derived bottom-up with realistic capture assumptions grounded in actual pipeline data. Score 2–3 if TAM is adequate but sources are weak, or if SAM/SOM logic has gaps but is directionally credible. Score 0–1 if TAM is too small for venture returns, sources are unreliable, or SOM assumes unrealistic market capture without evidence.*

### 4.2 — Market Timing 🚩 RED FLAG [0–5]

A clearly identifiable, externally driven structural shift — technological, regulatory, economic, or behavioral — creates a window of opportunity that did not exist 3 years ago. The timing thesis is anchored to specific, verifiable events (regulation passed or pending, technology cost curve inflecting, market structure changing) rather than internal milestones. The counterfactual test is satisfied: there is a clear reason why no one has already won this market.

Categories of timing drivers:
- **Regulatory shift** (e.g., EU EED, CSRD, AI Act, REACH — creating compliance demand)
- **Technology inflection** (e.g., cost/performance breakthrough enabling new use cases)
- **Market structure change** (e.g., supply chain reshoring, decarbonization mandates)
- **Behavioral shift** (e.g., enterprise willingness to adopt AI-driven processes)

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Ask the founders: "What changed in the last 2–3 years that makes this possible or necessary now?" The answer must cite specific, verifiable external events — not internal milestones.
- Independently verify each claimed timing driver: Is the regulation actually passed/pending? Is the technology cost curve actually inflecting? Find third-party data.
- Apply the counterfactual test: "Why hasn't someone already won this market?" If the market has existed for 10+ years without structural change, the timing thesis is weak.
- Check for the "technology adoption curve" position: Is the target customer segment early adopter, early majority, or late majority? This affects sales cycle and GTM strategy.

> *Red Flag triggers: No identifiable external timing driver. Market has existed for 10+ years without structural change — "Why hasn't someone already won this?"*

### 4.3 — Market Certainty [0–5]

Market demand is validated through strong, stage-appropriate signals as assessed per the Deep-Tech Matrix (cf. HTGF DTM, Berger/Joeressen/Fiebig 2025). Evidence goes beyond founder conviction, with demand confirmed through LOIs, paid pilots, early revenue, or competitor traction. Score based on the strength of demand signals:

| Signal Strength | Evidence | Typical Score |
|----------------|----------|:---:|
| Hypothetical | Founder conviction only | 1 |
| Researched | Third-party reports, expert interviews | 2 |
| Validated | Customer interviews confirm willingness to pay | 3 |
| Demonstrated | LOIs, paid pilots, early revenue | 4 |
| Proven | Recurring revenue, repeat purchases, competitor traction | 5 |

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Classify the company's current demand evidence into the table above. Be honest — verbal interest is not "Validated," and LOIs are not "Proven."
- Request evidence documents: LOIs (check if they are binding or non-binding), pilot agreements, purchase orders, invoices. Non-binding LOIs with no financial commitment are weaker signals than paid pilots.
- For "Researched" level: Who were the experts interviewed? How many? Are they potential buyers or just industry observers?
- For "Validated" level: Were willingness-to-pay interviews structured (e.g., Van Westendorp pricing, conjoint analysis) or casual conversations?
- Cross-reference with actual competitor traction: If competitors in the space have revenue, the market is at least partially proven — even if this specific company has limited traction.

### 4.4 — Regulation & ESG [0–5]

The regulatory environment creates a net positive force for the company, with regulation acting as a strong adoption tailwind at market level — forcing customers to act through compliance mandates, penalties, or structural requirements — while the compliance burden at company level is manageable with required certifications, approvals, and regulatory processes mapped with realistic timelines and budgets. No regulatory barrier blocks commercialization without a clear resolution path. The company has no material ESG risks (environmental, social, or governance) that could create regulatory, reputational, or financial exposure, and its mission positively aligns with impact investing criteria, unlocking access to dedicated impact funds, green bonds, or sustainability-linked financing. CSDDD supply chain cascading effects are understood and addressed.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**

**Regulatory Environment Assessment:**

**Market-level (external):** Does regulation create a forcing function for adoption (tailwind) or suppress demand (headwind)? EU-specific considerations: CSRD, EU Taxonomy, EED, AI Act, REACH, MDR, sector-specific directives.

**Company-level (internal):** What regulatory compliance burden does the company itself face? Assess: What certifications, approvals, or licenses are required to commercialize (CE, UL, FDA, AI Act compliance, export controls)? What is the timeline and cost to obtain them? Are there regulatory dependencies that could delay go-to-market or block market entry entirely?

- **Tailwinds:** Identify specific regulations that force the target customer to act. For each: When does it take effect? What is the penalty for non-compliance? How directly does the startup's product address the compliance requirement? The stronger the regulatory forcing function, the shorter the sales cycle.
- **Headwinds:** Could regulation suppress demand or restrict the startup's ability to operate? Common examples: EU AI Act compliance costs (estimated €160K–330K for high-risk AI systems), GDPR data processing restrictions, sector-specific licensing requirements.
- **EU AI Act (effective August 2025):** If the product involves AI, determine the risk classification (Minimal / Limited / High / Unacceptable). High-risk systems (estimated 33%+ of AI applications) require conformity assessments, risk management systems, and public database registration. Penalty exposure reaches €35M or 7% of global turnover for prohibited practices. Ask: "Has the company assessed its AI Act risk classification? What is the compliance plan and estimated cost?"
- **GDPR impact:** For data-intensive products: What personal data is collected? How is it processed? Where is it stored? Has a Data Protection Impact Assessment been conducted? GDPR compliance is a mandatory DD check for all European operations — non-compliance can block partnerships with enterprise customers.
- **Regulatory timeline mapping:** For each required certification/approval, map out: Application date → Expected approval date → Cost. Align with the use-of-proceeds plan (6.2) to ensure regulatory costs are budgeted and timelines are realistic.

**ESG Risk Screening & Impact Profile Assessment:**

- **Environmental risk:** Does the company's product or supply chain have material environmental impacts (emissions, waste, resource consumption, biodiversity)? Could upcoming regulation (CSRD, EU Taxonomy, CSDDD — transposition deadline July 2026) create compliance obligations or supply chain due diligence requirements?
- **CSDDD exposure:** The Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive requires supply chain due diligence for human rights and environmental impacts covering upstream AND downstream activities. Penalties reach up to 5% of worldwide net annual turnover. While currently targeting large companies (1,000+ employees), this cascades down supply chains — meaning startups that are suppliers to large companies will face compliance demands from their customers.
- **Social risk:** Are there labor practices concerns in the supply chain? Does the company's product raise ethical questions (e.g., surveillance, dual-use, bias in AI)? Is diversity represented in the founding team and hiring plan?
- **Governance risk:** Is the corporate governance structure sound? Are there related-party transactions, conflicts of interest, or governance gaps?
- **Positive impact alignment:** Does the company's mission align with ESG or impact investing criteria? Could this unlock access to dedicated impact funds, green bonds, or sustainability-linked financing? Is the impact measurable and reportable?
- **LP alignment:** For funds with ESG-conscious LPs: Does this investment support the fund's ESG reporting requirements and LP commitments?

> *Red Flag indicator: Product requires regulatory approval (e.g., FDA, CE for medical devices) with >24-month timeline and no approval strategy in place. Material ESG risk (e.g., supply chain human rights exposure, unresolved AI ethics) with no mitigation plan.*
>
> *Scoring guidance: Score reflects the weaker of (a) regulatory landscape (tailwinds vs. headwinds, compliance burden manageability) and (b) ESG risk profile (material risks identified, impact alignment potential). Score 5 if strong regulatory tailwind forcing adoption with lightweight compliance path, no material ESG risks, and strong positive impact alignment that opens additional funding channels. Score 3 if moderate regulatory tailwind with manageable compliance burden and manageable ESG risks with no impact upside. Score 0–1 if regulatory environment is a net headwind without mitigation, heavy compliance burden with uncertain timeline, or material ESG risks (e.g., supply chain human rights exposure, unaddressed AI ethics concerns) with no mitigation plan.*
>
> *Best practice (VentureESG): Tiered screening — high-level compliance check at pitch review, material ESG risk identification for potential investments, term sheet integration requiring DEI plans and ESG action plans.*

### 4.5 — Geographic Scalability [0–5]

The market opportunity scales beyond the initial DACH beachhead without fundamental product modification, and risks from export controls or market-specific certification barriers are mitigable. The product is globally applicable rather than inherently local, and the team has the language skills, network, or partner relationships to execute international expansion. For funds with a German/DACH focus: German nexus is maintained, while scalability beyond DACH is a value driver.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- Map the product's applicability by geography. Ask: "Which markets beyond DACH can you enter without fundamental product modification?" Document any market-specific customization needed (language, regulation, standards, certification).
- Identify geographic barriers: Is the product tied to a specific regulatory regime (e.g., German energy market rules)? Does it require local infrastructure, partners, or certifications in each new market?
- For hardware: Are there export control restrictions (ITAR, dual-use EU regulations)? Could the product be sold internationally without redesign for local standards?
- Assess international GTM readiness: Does the team have language skills, network, or partners for target expansion markets?

### 4.6 — Macro/Supply Chain Risk [0–5]

The company has minimal exposure to macro-economic shocks, geopolitical disruptions, and supply chain fragility — or has diversified supply chains with documented contingency plans for plausible scenarios. Critical components are not single-sourced from geopolitically sensitive regions, unit economics are resilient to energy and materials price fluctuations, and the business model does not collapse under plausible trade policy or currency scenarios. This assessment is especially critical for deep-tech companies with hardware components, international supply chains, or exposure to trade-sensitive sectors.

**How to assess (junior analyst checklist):**
- **Supply chain exposure:** Map the critical supply chain. Where are key components sourced? Are any sourced from geopolitically sensitive regions (China, Taiwan, Russia)? Could trade restrictions, tariffs, or sanctions disrupt supply? What is the lead time for critical components — and what happens if lead times double?
- **Energy and materials price sensitivity:** Does the company's unit economics depend on stable energy or raw material prices? What would a 2x increase in energy/materials costs do to gross margins?
- **Trade policy and sanctions risk:** Is the technology subject to export controls (dual-use, defense-related)? Could changes in trade policy (e.g., US-China decoupling, EU trade barriers) create headwinds or tailwinds?
- **Currency and interest rate exposure:** For companies with international revenue or costs: What is the FX exposure? For capital-intensive companies: How sensitive is the financial model to interest rate changes affecting cost of capital?
- **Geopolitical scenario planning:** Identify the 2–3 most plausible geopolitical scenarios that could impact the business (e.g., Taiwan conflict disrupting semiconductor supply, US-EU trade tensions, energy crisis). For each: What is the probability? What is the impact magnitude? What is the mitigation plan?

**Chapter 4 — Red Flags (automatic Pass triggers):**
- 🚩 No identifiable market timing driver ("Why Now?" cannot be articulated)
- 🚩 SAM too small for 10x fund-returning outcome (<€100M)
- 🚩 No external demand validation — thesis rests entirely on founder conviction
- 🚩 Regulatory environment is a headwind without mitigation strategy
- 🚩 Product requires regulatory approval with >24-month timeline and no approval strategy
- 🚩 Critical supply chain dependency on geopolitically unstable source with no alternative supplier
