### 8.1 — Valuation–Ownership Fit

☐ Checked and OK (✓) | ☐ Checked and Not OK (✗) | ☐ Open (○)

The deal economics work as an integrated equation: our ticket achieves target ownership (15–20%) at a pre-money valuation that is defensible given stage, traction, and DACH market comparables. Dilution path modeling through Series B shows healthy founder retention (>25% combined), preserving incentive alignment for the long term.

**Assessment guidance (junior analyst checklist):**

**Step 1: Ownership target check**

Does our ticket size, at the proposed pre-money valuation, yield our target ownership?

| Variable | Value | Check |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Fund ownership target | [15–20%] | Fund strategy constraint |
| Our ticket | €___ | Capital allocation constraint |

| Total round size | €___ | Incl. co-investors |
| **Our post-money ownership** | **___%** | **= Ticket / (Pre-money + Round)** |
| Ownership target achieved? | **[Yes / No]** | |

**Step 2: Valuation sanity check**

Is the implied pre-money defensible given stage, traction, and market comparables?

| Stage | Typical Pre-Money Range (DACH, 2025) | Valuation Driver |
|-------|:---:|---|
| Pre-Seed | €1.5M – €4M | Team + IP + early prototype |
| Seed | €3M – €10M | TRL 4–6 + pilot customers + IP |
| Series A | €8M – €25M | ARR + product-market fit + unit economics |

**Step 3: Dilution path modeling**

Model the cap table through the next 2 rounds. At typical Series A dilution (20–25%) and Series B dilution (15–20%), what does founder ownership look like?

| Scenario | Founder Ownership | Fund Ownership (no follow-on) | Fund Ownership (full pro-rata) |
|----------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Post this round | ___% | ___% | ___% |
| Post Series A (est.) | ___% | ___% | ___% |
| Post Series B (est.) | ___% | ___% | ___% |

**Checkpoint decision:**
- ✓ OK: Ownership target achieved at a valuation justified by traction, with healthy founder retention (>25% combined) through Series B
- ✗ Not OK: Ownership target missed by >5pp, OR valuation disconnected from stage/traction (down-round risk), OR cap table structurally broken (founders <50% post this round, or <25% projected at Series B)
- ○ Open: Ownership slightly below target but defensible given exceptional traction, OR minor cap table concerns with documented resolution plan

**Critical red flags:**
- Valuation implies ownership below fund minimum (e.g., <12%) at proposed ticket size
- Founders fall below 50% combined ownership post this round (common fund eligibility criterion)
- Cap table modeling shows founders below 25% by Series B — incentive alignment destroyed
- Valuation >2x above comparable recent rounds without 2x the traction to justify it

### 8.2 — Return Math & Exit Plausibility

☐ Checked and OK (✓) | ☐ Checked and Not OK (✗) | ☐ Open (○)

The 10x return math works: at our ownership percentage (accounting for dilution through subsequent rounds), the required exit enterprise value corresponds to an ARR target that is achievable within 5–7 years given market comparables, sector multiples, and the company's realistic growth trajectory. At least one high-plausibility exit pathway (trade sale, secondary, or IPO) supports this return scenario.

**Assessment guidance (junior analyst checklist):**

**10x Return Check:**

| Variable | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Our investment | €___ |
| Target return | 10x → €___ |
| Our ownership at exit (est., post-dilution) | ___% |
| **Required exit enterprise value** | **€___** |
| Implied ARR at exit (at ___x revenue multiple) | €___ |
| Is this ARR achievable in 5–7 years? | [Yes / Stretch / No] |

**Exit pathway assessment:**

| Exit Route | Timeline | Plausibility | Named Potential Acquirers |
|------------|:---:|:---:|---|
| Trade sale / strategic M&A | 5–7 years | [High / Medium / Low] | [Name 1, Name 2, Name 3] |
| Secondary / growth equity buyout | 5–8 years | [High / Medium / Low] | [Type of buyer] |
| IPO | 7–10 years | [High / Medium / Low] | [Min. ARR threshold] |

**Assessment steps:**
- Name 3–5 plausible strategic acquirers. For each: Why would they acquire? Have they acquired in this space? At what multiples?
- Research comparable exits: Crunchbase, PitchBook — document company, acquirer, revenue at exit, exit multiple, exit value.
- Run the 10x return math: At our ownership %, what exit EV is required? At sector-typical multiples, what revenue does that imply? Achievable in 5–7 years?
- **Exit probability weighting:** Assign rough probabilities to each pathway and calculate probability-weighted expected return.

**Checkpoint decision:**
- ✓ OK: Required exit ARR achievable within 5–7 years, with at least one high-plausibility exit pathway (3+ named acquirers with demonstrated M&A appetite)
- ✗ Not OK: Required ARR unrealistic given market size or growth trajectory, OR no identifiable strategic acquirers, OR sector too niche for IPO and no secondary market
- ○ Open: Required ARR is a stretch but achievable under bull case, OR exit pathway plausibility moderate but improvable with execution

> *Deep-tech reality: Trade sale to industrial strategics (Siemens, Bosch, Schneider, ABB, Honeywell) is the most common exit path for DACH deep-tech. IPO requires >€100M ARR and is rarely the base case at Seed.*

### 8.6 — BCSA Adoption Assessment

> *Assess whether the product achieves full BCSA (Better, Cheaper, Simpler, Available) alignment at the target value chain stage. See Appendix B for framework definitions, dimension details, and calibration case studies (Wiferion, Microshade).*
>
> **Core principle:** B+C (business case) alone are insufficient for adoption. If the product is Better and Cheaper but neither Simpler nor Available, adoption stalls — even in a rational market. S+A must match competitive parity.

**Assessment guidance (junior analyst checklist):**

**Step 1: B+C Assessment** (cross-reference from Chapters 5 & 6)

- B dimension: criterion 5.2 (10x Differentiation) + criterion 6.1 (Customer Demand / Pain Severity)
- C dimension: criterion 6.4 (USP Value Capture / Pricing Power) + criterion 6.5 (Unit Economics)
- **Critical check:** Is "Better" and "Cheaper" defined from the **economic decision-maker's** perspective — or the founders' technical perspective?

**Step 2: S Assessment (Simpler)**

- Is implementation effort ≤ the existing solution?
- Does the customer need infrastructure/process/personnel changes? If so, is effort quantified and vendor-supported?
- Can the customer simulate/forecast impact before purchase?
- Personnel training required? Compatible with existing/future technology?
- **Hardware deep-tech:** Same form factor / interface standard as existing solution?
- → See Appendix B: Wiferion case study for S-dimension calibration

**Step 3: A Assessment (Available)**

- Delivery at required volumes and timeframes?
- Installation, configuration, maintenance capabilities in place (internal or qualified partners)?
- Warranties and SLAs at competitive level?
- → New technology providers assume customers will "wait" given superior B+C — they will not.

**Step 4: Value Chain Analysis**

- Map the value chain: who are the actors at each stage?
- Assess BCSA fit at **each stage** separately — a product can be BCSA at one stage and not another
- Identify the stage with highest "Natural Relevance" (highest BCSA alignment = optimal beachhead)
- If not BCSA at any stage: can the value chain be disrupted through a new business model?
- → See Appendix B: Microshade case study for value chain disruption calibration

### 8.9 — Investment Thesis & Anti-Thesis

**Assessment guidance (junior analyst checklist):**

1. **Construct the Investment Thesis ("What Must Be True"):** Identify 3–4 conditions that MUST hold for this investment to deliver a 10x return. Each condition should map to one or more scored criteria or BCSA dimensions:
   - **Condition 1** should derive from the company's strongest scores (criteria 4–5) and the BCSA B+C strength. This is the "why this could be huge" statement.
   - **Condition 2** should address the adoption path — specifically BCSA S+A parity. If S or A have gaps (from 8.6), the condition should state what must happen for adoption to work.
   - **Condition 3** should address execution — the milestone plan from 8.5, path to next round from 7.4, and the DTM trajectory from 8.7.
   - Test each condition: Is it *falsifiable*? Can you define what evidence would prove it wrong within 12–18 months? If a condition can't be tested, it's too vague.

2. **Construct the Anti-Thesis ("The Bear Case"):** Write 3 counter-arguments a skeptical IC member would raise. Each must:
   - Link to a specific low-scored criterion (0–2) or a BCSA gap
   - Be *intellectually honest* — not a straw man. The best anti-theses are the ones that make you uncomfortable
   - Include an implicit "and here's why it might not be fixable" element
   - Common anti-thesis patterns: market timing is wrong (4.2), moat is thinner than it looks (5.3), unit economics don't converge (6.6), team has a critical gap (2.2), technology risk hasn't been addressed (3.1)

3. **Stress-test the thesis against the anti-thesis:** For each anti-thesis point, ask: "Does the investment thesis survive if this bear case materializes?" If any single anti-thesis point would kill the thesis entirely, that's a red flag requiring IC discussion.

4. **The "two-minute elevator" test:** Can you state the thesis in 2 sentences to a partner in the hallway? If not, the thesis isn't clear enough. Rewrite until it passes.

### 8.10 — Conviction Assessment & IC Vote

**Assessment guidance (junior analyst checklist):**

1. **Run the Red Flag Gate Check first:** Before forming any conviction, verify every 🚩 criterion. Pull scores from Chapters 2–7 for all red-flag criteria (2.1, 2.2, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 4.2, 5.2, 6.1, 6.2, 7.1) and the 8.1 Valuation–Ownership checkpoint. Any score of 0–1 on a 🚩 criterion triggers an automatic Pass recommendation — this is non-negotiable unless the IC explicitly overrides with documented rationale. If a red flag is triggered, your memo must prominently state this and the burden shifts to explaining why an override is warranted.

2. **Form conviction dimension by dimension:** For each of the 7 conviction dimensions (Team, Technology, Market, Deal Structure, Adoption/BCSA, Stage-Fit/DTM, External Risks), synthesize your assessment from the relevant chapter scores, BCSA findings, and DTM analysis:
   - **Team:** Average of Chapter 2 scores + qualitative judgment from reference checks (2.6) and coachability signals (2.4)
   - **Technology:** Average of Chapter 3 scores + external validation signals (3.5) + obsolescence risk (3.6)
   - **Market:** Average of Chapter 4 scores, with extra weight on timing (4.2) and market certainty (4.3)
   - **Deal Structure:** Summary of 8.1–8.5 checkpoints. Any ✗ should pull conviction toward Bearish
   - **Adoption (BCSA):** Direct from 8.6 assessment. Full BCSA = Bullish. B+C without S+A = Neutral to Bearish. Not BCSA = Bearish to Extremely Bearish
   - **Stage-Fit (DTM):** Direct from 8.7 assessment. On-track trajectory = Bullish. Minor gaps = Neutral. Misaligned or "No Money Land" adjacent = Bearish
   - **External Risks:** Synthesize from 8.8 threat matrix. High-likelihood / high-impact unmitigable risks pull toward Bearish

3. **Arrive at the Analyst Verdict:** Map the conviction pattern to the 6-point verdict scale (see Appendix V). Use these anchors:
   - *STRONG INVEST:* Bullish or Extremely Bullish on 5+ dimensions, no red flags, no Bearish dimensions. Rare — 1-2 per year.
   - *INVEST:* Bullish on 4+ dimensions, remaining dimensions Neutral, no red flags. You would put personal money in.
   - *LEAN INVEST:* Bullish on 3+ dimensions, some open questions remain. If management answers from Chapter 1 come back positive, this moves to INVEST.
   - *LEAN PASS:* Mixed signals — some Bullish, some Bearish. Interesting but not compelling enough today. Worth tracking.
   - *PASS:* Bearish on 2+ dimensions, or one critical dimension Extremely Bearish. Significant concerns unlikely to be resolved.
   - *STRONG PASS:* Extremely Bearish on 2+ dimensions, or fundamental structural problem (broken economics, team red flags, no market). Don't waste IC time.

4. **Write the verdict rationale in one sentence:** "This is a [VERDICT] and not [adjacent verdict] because [single most decisive factor]." This forces clarity. If you can't write this sentence, your conviction isn't clear enough.

5. **Prepare IC presentation:** Organize the key discussion points for the IC meeting. Anticipate the 3 toughest questions IC members will ask. For each: prepare the data-backed answer AND the honest "here's what we don't know" caveat. IC members respect analysts who know the limits of their analysis.
